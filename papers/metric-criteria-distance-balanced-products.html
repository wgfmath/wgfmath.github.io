Abstract

This paper is motivated by two explicit problems raised in the study of distance-balanced variants of graphs. Abedi, Alaeiyan, Hujdurovic and Kutnar asked for a structural understanding of quasi-lambda-distance-balanced direct products, while Kutnar and Miklavic asked for a characterization of nicely distance-balanced vertex-transitive graphs. We solve both problems by identifying the metric obstruction in each setting.

For direct products, we show that the relevant distance is governed by the synchronization of even and odd walks in the two factors. More precisely, we prove a parity-sensitive distance formula for G x H in terms of the shortest even and odd walks in G and H. This yields a necessary and sufficient factor-wise criterion for G x H to be quasi-lambda-distance-balanced. The criterion becomes explicit for products Km,n x H, where it is expressed through two arc invariants of H. As consequences, we obtain concrete infinite families such as Km,n x C2r+1 and Km,n x Kq.

For nicely distance-balanced graphs, we settle the vertex-transitive case. We prove that a finite connected vertex-transitive graph is nicely distance-balanced if and only if the number of vertices equidistant from the two ends of an edge is independent of the edge. This turns the problem into an edge-orbit invariant and leads to local, Cayley graph and Cartesian-product criteria. In particular, we construct infinite families of non-bipartite vertex-transitive nicely distance-balanced graphs that are not edge-transitive, showing that earlier edge-transitive sufficient conditions are far from necessary.

Together, these results give two complementary metric reductions: quasi-balance of direct products is controlled by parity synchronization, whereas nice balance in vertex-transitive graphs is controlled by the equidistance coordinate of the edge profile.
